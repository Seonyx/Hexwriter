## SECTION 2: THEMATIC MANDATE

This is the primary purpose of Draft 2. Every chapter must serve it.

### 2.1 The Central Theme

The mandatory revival system — the corporate infrastructure that brings people back after death — is not background texture. It is the spine of the story. Draft 2 makes this explicit from the first page.

Revival is tiered by wealth. Your coverage determines how many times you come back, how well, and at what cost. This is the class struggle at the heart of *The Chilli Racers*: in a world where combustion engines are banned and illegal racing is the last form of unsponsored freedom, the rich can race like video game characters — crashing, dying, respawning, crashing again — while the poor must be smarter, more precise, more creative, because they cannot afford to make the same mistake twice.

**The moral of the story is this:** intelligence, care, and solidarity beat recklessness backed by money. The tortoise wins. The hares crash out.

This is a quietly pro-universal-healthcare story. It should never lecture. It should demonstrate.

### 2.2 Coverage Tiers

Establish this vocabulary consistently across all chapters. Do not explain it in exposition — reveal it through behaviour, advertising, environment, and consequence.

| Tier | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Apex** | Corporate top tier. Effectively unlimited annual revival events. Full neural restoration. Rapid recovery (days, not weeks). Private clinical facilities. The very wealthy. Khoury, Whitmore. |
| **Premium** | Upper-middle. 6–8 revival events per contract year. Good recovery rates, minor residual effects possible. The comfortable professional class. |
| **Standard** | Working class with some safety net. 3–4 revivals per contract year. Recovery takes weeks. Neurological side effects are documented but quietly suppressed in marketing. Cage is on Standard. He has used 2 of his 3 remaining events before the story begins. |
| **Basic** | Bare minimum. 1–2 revival events per contract year. Slow, incomplete recovery. Permanent injury risk is real. Flynn is on Basic — one event remaining. Wrench was on Basic. |
| **St. Mercy's** | Not a revival tier. A charity hospital network that provides conventional medicine only — no revival, no neural restoration. What happens when the Basic plan runs out, or when the paperwork takes too long, or when the ambulance from the wrong company arrives. The floor of the system. |

Revival events are tracked by implant. The counter is visible to the bearer and can be shared with emergency services. In practice, wealthy racers never check their counter. Poor racers check it the way you check fuel levels: habitually, anxiously, quietly.

### 2.3 Cage's Secret

Cage entered the story with 5 Standard revival events remaining. The opening crash of Chapter 1 uses one, reducing him to **2 remaining**. He knows this. He tells no one. His recklessness is not born of invincibility — it is born of not caring about the counter. He believes freedom is worth dying for permanently. This is his tragedy and his flaw. He is the hare not because he is privileged but because he has philosophically surrendered his caution.

### 2.4 Flynn's Hidden Stakes

Flynn has Basic coverage with **1 revival event remaining** for the contract year. He does not advertise this. It is why he drives the way he drives — careful, considered, calculating — not because he is timid but because he has done the arithmetic and the arithmetic says: survive. This is his strength, not his weakness.

### 2.5 Wrench's Prior Damage

Wrench has already used his 2 Basic revival events. He is currently unrevivable — if he dies, St. Mercy's is his only option. The "wrongness" in his behaviour that colleagues sense (slight left-hand tremor, occasional blank periods, sensitivity to bright light) is residual neurological damage from an incomplete Basic revival. The crew knows something is wrong. Nobody names it. This knowledge makes his injury in Chapter 15 devastating: he is going in with no safety net and everyone in the room understands that.

### 2.6 The Billionaire Antagonists

**Reed Whitmore** — the primary antagonist and Dick Dastardly foil. Made his fortune in solar energy infrastructure after the combustion ban; he is, with some precision, the man who profited most from the law that made this race necessary. He enters La Carrera del Chile Rojo as sport, as spectacle, as a dare to himself. He has been revived 4 times in competitive settings. His team operates a branded mobile Apex revival unit at the race — a gleaming van with a logo and a medical team — that costs more than the Compound's annual budget. He is not a cartoon villain. He is charming, curious, and utterly without imagination about what life is like without his coverage. He would be genuinely surprised to learn that the people he is racing against cannot simply get back up.

**Khoury** (established in Draft 1, Ch. 13–14) — Premium coverage, wealthy collector, the target of the Phoenix heist. His presence as a race entrant is confirmed in Ch. 11 of this draft. He has a score to settle with the Compound crew. This adds a personal threat to the class threat.

Other wealthy entrants appear at the race grid (Ch. 17 onwards) and can be sketched briefly — the point is the contrast in setup, composure, and equipment between the moneyed teams and the home team.

### 2.7 The Tortoise and the Hare

The race (Chapters 18–22) must dramatise this principle in real time:

- Wealthy racers crash and are revived and continue. They race recklessly because the cost of recklessness is zero to them.
- Flynn drives with ferocious precision. Every decision is made with the knowledge that he has one respawn. He cannot afford to guess. He becomes faster through exactness, not bravado.
- Cage races as if he is Apex when he is not. He burns his second-to-last revival event in the race. He wins sections, leads sections, drives beautifully. And then he crashes for the last time, on empty.
- Flynn does not win because he is lucky. He wins because he was better. The system designed to give the rich an infinite advantage is defeated not by equalising resources but by excellence.

### 2.8 The Quiet Message

Do not editorialize. Do not have characters deliver speeches about universal healthcare. The story makes its argument through the architecture of what happens: the people with money crash and come back; the people without money have to think. The epilogue (Chapter 24) shows the race footage repurposed as content — the deaths, the crashes, the drama — commodified and sold, the system entirely unchanged. Flynn drives away alive. That is the ending. The reader can do the rest.

## SECTION 3: WORLDBUILDING RULES

Apply these consistently. Gemini must not contradict them or vary them between chapters.

- **Revival is corporate, not medical.** The companies are never named — they are implied through brand aesthetics, advertising, and the bureaucratic language of implant notifications. Avoid naming a single revival corporation.
- **Implants are universal.** Everyone has them. They provide navigation, commercial overlays, health monitoring, and coverage status. The wealthy have premium implants; the poor have basic models prone to ads at inopportune moments. The implant coverage counter is formatted as a simple number in the corner of the user's vision.
- **Combustion engines are banned.** The world runs on solar and electric. The cars in this story are illegal relics. Their drivers are outlaws not for any political reason but simply because they love what the world decided to bury.
- **The ban on combustion is primarily enforced via insurance.** Drive a combustion vehicle and your coverage is void. This is the quiet genius of the corporate system: they don't need police. They just exclude.
- **Revival is not resurrection.** It is expensive emergency medicine using advanced neural preservation and cardiac restoration. The character is the same person. They remember everything. But recovery is not clean — particularly at Standard and below. There is always a cost. The Apex marketing materials don't show the cost.
- **St. Mercy's** is visible as signage and as a last resort in conversation. Characters with used-up Basic plans reference it the way people reference bankruptcy: quietly, without looking at each other.
- **Simile discipline.** Draft 1 contains 200+ instances of the "like a [thing]" simile construction. Draft 2 should reduce this to no more than 40 across the full manuscript. Cut freely. Replace with concrete description, action, or silence where appropriate.

## SECTION 4: CHARACTER NOTES FOR DRAFT 2

**Flynn** — Basic coverage, 1 event remaining. Cautious not from fear but from mathematics. His city-life flashback (planted in Ch. 2) shows a life inside the system: a desk job, a covered implant, a manageable fear of dying. He left that life but brought the arithmetic with him. He is the protagonist. He should win.

**Cage** — Standard coverage, 2 events remaining at story start, 1 remaining after Ch. 1. Charismatic, self-destructive, genuinely gifted. His recklessness is philosophy, not ignorance — he has decided the counter doesn't matter. This makes him admirable and wrong in equal measure. He is not the villain. He is the cautionary tale the story loves.

**Diesel** — Her coverage tier is never stated. She never discusses it. This is deliberate. When characters probe, she changes the subject. The reader can infer. She is the most tactically intelligent member of the crew.

**Birdie** — Basic coverage, terrified of his counter, masks it with bravado and dark humour. His jokes about dying are jokes with a tremor in them. He should feel like someone who has done the math and decided not to look at the answer.

**Wrench** — Coverage exhausted. Post-revival neurological damage already present. No safety net. His arc in Ch. 14–15 is the class theme made flesh.

**Don Castellanos** — The chilli baron. Premium coverage. Old money from before the ban. He sponsors the race and the home team's entry because he remembers racing when it was equal, before the tier system. He does not fully understand the Compound crew's precarity; he thinks sponsorship fixes it. It doesn't fix Wrench. This matters.

**Reed Whitmore** — Apex coverage, 4 prior racing revival events, racing is sport and dare and spectacle. Physically fit, well-presented, genuinely competitive. He is not gratuitously cruel — he simply operates in a different reality, one where crashing is a story you tell at dinner.

**Khoury** — Premium coverage, angry about the heist, personally invested in the race as revenge territory.

Sections labelled **"Qwen tooling steps"** contain file I/O and XML operations — execute these directly as the primary Gemini Flash agent.

### 6.2 Rate Limiting

- Wait **60 seconds** after each Qwen prose task completion before starting the next chapter.
- Gemini Flash tool calls (file read, write, validate) run immediately — no delay required.
- On Qwen task failure: log to `~/chilli-rewrite/logs/taskNN.log`, wait 10 minutes, retry once. If retry fails, log `FAILED_PERMANENT` and continue to the next task.
- Never run chapter tasks in parallel.

### 6.3 Directory Convention

```
~/chilli-rewrite/
  source/          ← unzipped source export (READ ONLY — never write here)
  output/          ← chapter-by-chapter output tree
  logs/            ← one log file per task: task01.log, task02.log, etc.
```

### 6.4 XML Integrity Rules

- All output XML must be UTF-8. Smart quotes, em-dashes, and accented characters (especially in Spanish words from the race) must be written as literal UTF-8, not XML entities.
- Validate every chapter file with `xmllint --noout` before writing the Discord notification.
- Paragraph count check: always use `grep -c '<para '` (with trailing space) — never `grep -c '</para>'`. Self-closing empty paras produce no closing tag and will be missed.
- `seq` attributes on `<para>` elements must be renumbered in steps of 1000 in the output.
- `draft-created="1"` and `draft-modified="1"` apply to any source para that is carried over unchanged. Any para that is rewritten or new gets `draft-created="2"` (if new) or `draft-modified="2"` (if rewritten from a source para). `modified-by="ai"` on all modified/new paras. `modified-date` must be set to the actual ISO 8601 datetime at the moment Qwen writes each output file — use `$(date -u +%Y-%m-%dT%H:%M:%SZ)` to generate this value dynamically. Do not hardcode a date.
- The `sed` sweep in the final task (`s/draft="1"/draft="2"/g`) is safe — `<draft number="1"` in the versioning block uses `number=` not `draft=` and will not be caught. Verify after sweep that versioning entries are intact.

### 6.5 Para ID Rules for Merged Chapters

When source chapters are merged (ch03+ch04 → output ch03; ch05+ch06 → output ch04), paragraph IDs from both source files must be deduplicated in the output. Where a source para is carried forward, keep its pid. Where a new para is written, generate a new pid using the output chapter ID prefix and the character set `ABCDEFGHJKLMNPQRSTUVWXYZ23456789`. Never reuse a pid that appeared in either source chapter.
